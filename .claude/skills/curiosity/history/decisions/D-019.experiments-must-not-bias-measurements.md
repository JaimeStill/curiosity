# D-019 — Experiments must not bias measurements via simplifications — 2026-05-06

**Scope**: project

**Context**: While implementing the sparsesetmap iterator during the storage-comparison experiment, I proposed skipping the per-row membership probe in `Next()` — the rationale being that the iteration baseline workload spawns every entity with every queried component, so the failed-membership branch is never taken at runtime, so the check could be deferred until later workloads needed it. The user pushed back: skipping a cost that real sparse-set implementations always pay would make sparseset's iteration baseline look faster than any production implementation could possibly achieve, biasing the cross-backend comparison and producing measurements that don't predict real behavior. The iterator was redesigned to pre-resolve dense indices in `Next()` with the explicit membership check, and that's how today's three-way iteration data was produced. Without the correction, sparsesetslice's "matches archetype" finding would have been an artifact of measurement bias rather than a real result.

**Decision**: Experiments faithfully reproduce the cost profile of real implementations of whatever they're measuring, even when the chosen workload doesn't exercise that cost at runtime. Concessions made for code-simplicity that compress the measured cost relative to production code are not acceptable. This applies regardless of whether the simplification is documented — measurement-bias-by-omission is still measurement bias.

**Reasoning**: The `experiments/` directory exists to provide accurate baselines for engine-design decisions (D-012). An experiment that under-reports a backend's cost makes that backend look better on paper than it would be in production — exactly the kind of "data point" that misleads rather than informs. Honesty in measurement is the whole product the experiment is producing; everything else (code reuse, brevity, conventions exemption) is subordinate to it. The principle is general — it applies to any future experiment, not just ECS storage. The temptation to simplify will recur whenever a code path looks unreachable for the current workload; the rule is the discipline that overrides the temptation.

This decision sits cleanly alongside D-012's conventions exemption: experiments are exempt from style and ceremony (godoc, unit tests, doc.go), not from measurement validity. Code can be quick and dirty without being measurement-misleading. The boundary is whether the simplification distorts what the experiment is producing as output.

**Alternatives**: (1) Skip the cost, document the simplification, and have downstream readers account for it during interpretation — rejected because (a) downstream readers of the data don't necessarily read the implementation, (b) the "account for it" step is rarely actually performed even when the disclaimer is in place, (c) the simplification can compound non-obviously across multiple workloads or backends, and (d) the experiment's measurements themselves are the artifact future readers will cite, regardless of disclaimers attached. (2) Skip the cost and rely on later workloads to surface the discrepancy — rejected because the iteration baseline will typically be re-run as backends evolve, perpetuating the bias session over session. (3) Treat measurement integrity as conventions-grade rather than decision-grade and put it in `code/conventions.md` — rejected because conventions are exempt for experiments per D-012, and the principle is exactly what the exemption must NOT extend to.
